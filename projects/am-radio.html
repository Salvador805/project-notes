AM Radio

Overview

This was a class project, in which we tried creating two am radios. One in which we use a 555 timer to create a periodic square wave as a our carrier wave and a sine wave from a function generator as our message wave. The other am radio would be using another function generator for our carrier wave instead of the 555 timer. The modulated wave would be transmitted via an LED and on the receiving end it would use a phototransistor to pick up the wave. We used a jFET as our modulating device which after transmitting we would be able to tune into the frequency by using an LC resonator, then we would demodulate it by using a peak detector. The circuit can be seen above. By creating this circuit on a breadboard I was only able to get to transmitting it to the LED, but the demodulation did not end up working due to lack of time in class. I continued this project on LTspice and finished it on there. I had to do multiple changes to the breadboard circuit I mentioned above in Figure 1 and 2 to make it successful.

Note: The LTspice circuit was built only around the 555 timer as my carrier, I didn't recreate my breadboard circuit in which my carrier was another sine wave.

    • One Challenge

    Relearning the basics was one of the hardest things I had to do in this project. I thought I knew what I was doing during my breadboard circuit building process, but turns out I didn't actually know. I was following a general schematic in which I thought I knew what each component did. I realized I actually didn't know the fundamentals enough when I tried transitioning into my LTspice circuit using the same circuit from my breadboard. As I was building my way up to the circuit on my breadboard I had the opportunity to mess around with resistor values, capacitor values to see if my wave would change how I expected it. Building from the ground up helped me understand more importantly what each component did rather than just paying attention to a single component like the jFET. This was definitely a big growing experience. I learned that understanding the fundamentals of a project is something you have to get done at the beginning of a project. I have attached my AM Radio notebook for my breadboard circuit, notes I took during the LTspice process, and my AM Radio overleaf write-up.

    Some Findings

    As you know the schematic depicted in Figure 1 and 2 was the one I followed during my breadboard circuit. In this section we can see the actual LTspice circuit. One big change I had to do was add in a resistor in series with op-amps because LTspice treats this op-amps as ideal. This meaning that they had a special characteristic where they provide infinite current. It is described in more detail in the LTspice circuit attached. Another big finding was the fact the frequencies I chose which were 90Hz for my message, and 969Hz for my carrier frequency. Using these two values actually made a somewhat big impact on my spice model. I ended up learning that using LC resonator as my tuner would best fit instances where my carrier frequency is in the kHz and MHz range. In my circuit it proved no benefit, so I took it out. Another problem was the fact that because I chose this frequencies, I wasn't able to completely cutoff the high frequencies at the end( shown on the figure provided). I even tried using a two pole RC filter to increase my rolloff rate.It proved beneficial but it just wasn't enough. The fix for this would be to choose a better carrier/message frequency combination such that we aren't at the minimum requirement of having the carrier frequency being at least 10 times that of the message frequency. This would create a big enough separation between the two frequencies such that I can cleanly cutoff the high frequency noise. At the end we were able to get a somewhat clean and understandable output.
